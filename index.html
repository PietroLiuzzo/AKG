Photo Collection Data as Graph? Connecting with Databases of Inscriptions from Rome

While the Fotothek has many images of Inscriptions in Rome or Drawings of inscriptions in Rome, Epigraphic Databases contain editions of inscriptions in Rome with images.

Following on the workshop goal and input, I will try to explore the state-of-the-art of the data about one item and look at its multidisciplinary representation in the data available on the web, so primarily to those who do not have a library card and may relay only on web resources. I believe the available material has all the potential for a really multidisciplinary understanding of each piece and aspect of the city of Rome, but that collaboration is far from being implemented enough to allow the missing bit to enact that data, which is scholarly and research-based connections within argumentative narratives.

I claim that there is no technological obstacle to that, and instead it can be achieved easily and is at the hand of everyone.

It is a much more challenging task than implementing large infrastructures which are then empty of contents.

The role of the interpreter of historical sources and the knowledge related to it, which allows to connect different materials, are largely missing in web resoruces. I will try to show something which is not new, that the two worlds of knowledge, that of printed materials and the one building up in the web are similar and complementary: while both suffer the same issues (accessibility, quality control, style) they both build knowledge by linking. Linkage in both ways from the web-based-and-digital world to the printed-material world are bidirectionaly faulty and hard to make without specific expertiese.

Once more the researcher (scholarly or not) is the missing link, not the software or the tools. Collaborative tools exist and can be used. For a new understanding we badly need a new generation of well trained researchers of many kinds open to the technical languages as well as to the aesthetics, who are supported and recognized for their contribution to collaborative resources like the web in any of its parts.

With the information we have we could direct links and enhance discoverability of many other available resources, which are available online for the same object. A meaningful link is much more valuable than a large, closed, database of informations, however rich.

So, let me try to think like Google's Algorithm for a moment and follow links to make an example of what kind of networks and connections can be extracted to read an object from the city of Rome.

Collecting data

Fotothek Foto - bhpd71012z: Statuensockel mit Inschrift des Caius Cestius - Frontseite mit Inschrift Viewer

I will start from a record in the Fototeca of the Bibliotheca Hertziana, follow its link into a website of the Musei Capitolini and then move to the Epigraphic Datanbase Rome, the largest existing academic corpus of inscriptions from Rome online, including more than 105 thousands digital editions of epigraphic texts. and follow links from there to explore how much information can be actually connected to get the best possible knowledge of a single object.

My argument is that there is more real and direct value for the exploration of the network of informations about the city of Rome in a argumentative knowledge graph than there can be from the extraction of value from quantitavely collected data. This is because of the fragmentary nature of the data and because the actual available data is scanty and clashes in front of the bibliographic barrier, aka the innaccessibility of quality full texts of publications quoted as unstructured data.

This presentation is in itself an example of an argumentative knowledge graph (AKG) as it embedds RDFa in the HTML source. A simple tool like RDFa Play can be used to visualize the data as a graph and it can be imported as is into a triplestore. Here I parse the HTML with vis.js and produce the Network with the same library.

Any scientific contribution is per sé a knowledge graph. This one simply attempts to make that known to the web by using one of its standard technologies, RDFa.

The restoration of the Pyramid - Context

To give some context, while the Pyramid, dated by this inscriptions to between 18 and 12 BCE, and once thought to be the tomb of Romolus or Remus, the city wall was there since Aurelian built it in 272 CE. The system of towers was added only recently by the precedessor of Alexander VII, Urban VIII Barberini (See: Piero Maria Lugli Urbanistica di Roma 1998, p. 46 e 108-110).

Searching for Pyramids and Alexander VII one quickly meets the story of the Duke of Cérqui who issued a request to build a pyramid in reparation for an accident with the Course Guards. The Internet archive has extensive reading about this in The history of the popes, from the close of the middle ages : drawn from the secret Archives of the Vatican and other original sources; from the German by Pastor, Ludwig, freiherr von, 1854-1928, p.110-111

It is also uncomplicated to find an image of this other Pyramid and its inscription.

Vocabularies

A further enterprise which replicates the contents of the Fotothek must be named, for its large effort in harmonization and alignement of vocabularies, which is the Pharos Artresearch platform, although still in beta release.

Here we need to search for Caius Cestius to get to our item, but we do get there.

Matching ends, aligning vocabularies

While Pharos Vocabularies actually link up the resources they align, the EAGLE Vocabularies leave this to their users. Let us see how much connection we can get and especially if we do learn something new from this connections, except for opening up to more examples of entities tagged in the same way, which is of course already useful for some purposes.

While the entities for Photographer, Current Location and Institution are a very useful local navigation hint, the other terms, which are present in other resources offer ways to connect and align.

Pharos Sockel is aligned to Getty AAT 300264092, an Object Facet but has no declared direct correspondence. The AAT value for Sockel provided by the MIDAS vocabulary is 300080499 as we have seen and the EAGLE Vocabulary for Basis aligns to https://archwort.dainst.org/thesaurus/de/vocab/?tema=1484 , which cannot be retrieved. But we know that EDR and Europeana as well as the Fotothek and Pharos refer to the same conceptual thing. We know we are speaking of the same thing, and the SKOS version of the MIDAS vocabulary adds the missing alignment to GND.

Skulptur is not used in the Epigraphic databases but both Pharos and the MIDAS Vocabulary refer to the GND entity. For Pharos however this is identical to AAT 300047090, while MIDAS claims is only related.

Marmor is aligned in EDR with a concept from an unreacheable resource of the DAI, https://archwort.dainst.org/thesaurus/de/vocab/?tema=2383. In Pharos it is considered identical to AAT 300011443. and so it is by the MIDAS Vocabulary. We can safely say the same for the EDR and EDH.

The discordant typology of inscription is relevant only in the epigraphic database.

Unfortunately, if we were hoping to see the ends join up via the vocabularies, we have not found what we where looking for, or at least not quite.

Vocabularies offer however many further ways to navigate related information, whose relevance is limited for the purpose of this paper.

RDFa encoding

So, what have we learned?

This article is also a dataset. While writing it directly as HTML, I have embedded triples in RDFa for all relevant statments to build a network of web resources related to the object of enquiry.

RDFa can be encoded in many ways, here I have encoded only the following formats, to facilitate myself.

@resource @typeof @resource @property @about @property text() @property @href @property @src

You can see here the network of statements availabe (about 500). The network formed by the argument in this contribution.

All triples here are extracted and serialised as nodes and edges.

Semantic Layers

But what is RDFa encoded? It could be anything actually. Here I have identified Digital Objects and their relations to Research Organization. I have tried to be systematic about the realations among the entities and I have provided relations to the main available vocabularies. I have added few statements about Events occurring to objects and Transformations undergone by Objects.

Beside more known Conceptual reference models, vocabularies and ontologies, I have used properties and classes from the ontology of La Syntax du Codex, which I developed some years ago and tested in another research environment. This is a structural guide to the description of manuscripts. Its applicability to any written artefact has been clear since its publication, and here I have applied it in a very simple way to the two statue bases and their inscriptions.

Another less known model, the so-called epigraphic ontology, for the description of inscription is the result of a large collaboration, which I helped write up some years ago, and is tailored to the need of inscriptions. This helps especially in the distinction of units related to text and support, with a core descriptive distinction based on the recognition of the binary nature of an inscribed object as text on a support and as support with a text.

Additionally I have added connections among the described resources, and alignment of the vocabulary.

If the statements in the form of triples or those in the form of sentences have had priority in this process, I could not say at the moment.

Networks and Graphs

A Graph with all nodes in this presentation as above is however not very useful, or readable. It may convey a sense of unity and coherence, but does not tell us more than what the data has already said, especially in this case, where the triples reproduce the content of the argument.

Let me show you instead two graphs, one with only the relations which can be extracted from the data available online, and one with only the triples which are added here, excluding also all those triples which are functional to the presentation (title, author) and not related to its contents. I have done this classifying the triples with a @data-type equal to akg for what has been added here, equal to source for what was available elsewhere and is only reproduced here, and equal to functional for the rest. I could not think of another way to do this, given I have used the same vocabularies as my sources.

Cleaning the Network

Here we can observe something which we knew already: the content of a contribution is coherent (hopefully), and the resources out there are only some times kept together by sporadic links.

If we also clean up relations which are not directly relevant semantically to a researcher, e.g. urls, we have very little, but a quite clear picture of what resources we actually have.

Taking classification away (is a... @typeof) we see how also keywords get isolated in the network. I am not sure about the relevance of this in network analysis. It certainly lowers my expectations from vocabulary-based alignments.

Not much of a Network without the argument connecting the pieces

What we take home from this reduction process is that all what really matters are links. Explicit and direct ones. The single link added by virtue of sending an email was more important than any long process to integrate resources in platforms and aggregators.

But what does these informations actually tell us alone? without those added information, without the additional links from the current paper, etc.?

We can see that Epigraphic databases are more and better connected among themselves, yet there is only that one link joining the lonely Fotothek to them. And another lonely link to join the Fotothek to the resources of the Capitolini.

A user landing on the Capitolini Page would find only the information in there.

A user landing on the Fotothek would be able to get to the Capitolini as well.

A user of EDR would find enough links to make its way to all available materials.

Why? Because they have more links pointing outside of their confort zone, quite simply.

We need links, to join up in the wider world wide network. And not internal links, links of others, reaching out and letting the users reach out to other relevant resources. So, I have landed to another obvious point, which I need to constantly remind myself of: we need to do research, get interesting research questions, write them up and publish them. Yes, just as we have done for centuries before the web.

But what can we do to facilitate that? We cannot expect anyone to be crazy enought to write up papers in HTML+RDFa. The following is my wishlist.

  • Link outside of our resources
  • Digitize more, diverse material, openly and accessibly.
  • Encode bibliographies fully, making books and article reachable as quickly as possible.
  • Be linkable, exposing URLs to resources as explicitly as possible, and avoiding unreadable addresses.
  • Identify areas of lacking information opening up interaction possibilities like crowd/expert sourcing.

Writing and linking

Stories and Storytelling for the web have been a variously aknowledged reality for many years. But resarchers struggle to aknowledge the inherent similarity of their products to a blog. Let alone allowing those contents to be editable by others.

If it is impossible to overcome this barrier, we can work the other way around and insist on web-based collaborative products which are better than their counterparts.

Technicalities overwhel the effort in many ways and hinder it largely.

If ChatGPT cannot write this paper, it does not mean we should not help it be able to do so. Indeed we need to produce enough qualitative arguments to balance the automatic production of endless useless contents. So, we will help ourselves by helping the machines access data and avoid confusion.

Focusing on writing tools and methods of research is therefor at the bottom line essential.

It is an old research question: the argument first or the data first? Or do they chease each other constantly? Writing up following an order, some logically earlier entities come up later.

RDF data is slower and needs more precision when written up, then a normal argument would require. When we speak and write we adapt and understand, we let places open up questions. But often if a node is needed by a model and there is no sentence for it in the argument, that sentence may be useful to enhance the reading clarity.

Perhaps our novel editorial enterprises may do without a specialist of the color of prints, but will need at all stages an ontology specialist to help with the modeling of the argument.

Data alone, especially in graph formats is illegible and hence unanderstandable, without sequences. It organization is non sequential and has its advantages in that but sequences are only possible at one or two levels, because each possible path may not be precluded. Arguments through data help greatly and uniquely the accessibility of data.

Argument-based knowledge graphs, small, specific, and standardized in their vocabularies, are in my opinion a good way to let the consistency and richness of the web of data for Rome but for any given context of interest, expand as the web allows it to do.
